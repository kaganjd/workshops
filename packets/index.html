the internet is made of data packets

#goals: get familiar with some of the core parts of internet infrastructure like routers, servers and ISPs; know what a data packet is and how websites get to computers; have enough of an understanding of packet sniffing to be able to explore independently.

duration: 1.5 hours

prerequisites: none

materials needed: internet access; at least one computer; tactical tech's deck of internet infrastructure cards; a packet sniffing application (download wireshark or herbivore).

lens

activists and organizers use the internet all the time‚Äîto post pictures, to collect information from folks who come to meetings, to plan actions. but many of us don't know how the internet actually works or who holds power to control the flow of data across the network.

packet sniffing is a small way to take a peek under the hood at the very big system that is the internet.

exercise: build the internet

  1. break out into groups
  2. each groups gets a deck of the tactical tech internet infrastructure cards
  3. spend some time laying out the cards according to how you think a website gets to your computer
  4. come back together and let's discuss: how did this feel? did you know more or less than you thought?

website

the collection of files‚Äîfrom style files, scripts, images, and plain text‚Äîthat you request from the server and that are ultimately rendered on your computer by your browser.

router

the device that forwards, or routes, data packets along to where they‚Äôre supposed to go based on the addresses in the packet headers.

server

the computer that hosts websites and makes them publicly available through a URL. the server responds to client requests.

any computer can be both a server or client‚Äîit just depends what role the computer is playing. is the computer serving files or requesting files?

when people use the metaphor of 'the cloud' to talk about file storage, they're talking about a server or just "another person's computer."

internet service provider

there are many kinds of internet service providers, but in this case we're talking about access providers. these are the companies that install cable in your neighborhood and often supply your router when you set up your internet in your home. ISPs hold a lot of power because they physically control the flow of data.

for example, whistleblower mark klein described how the NSA was collecting internet communications by interfering in an ISP's physical infrastructure.

ISPs are also allowed to sell our browsing data to private companies.
